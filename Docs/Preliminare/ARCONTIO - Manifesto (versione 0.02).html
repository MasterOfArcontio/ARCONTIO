MANIFESTO — ITCH.IO

ARCONTIOA social simulation where NPCs act on what they know and remember, not on what is actually happening

Most colony simulators offer a god's-eye view of the world. You see everything. You know everything. Your colonists, your citizens — they are extensions of your will, executing plans handed down from above.

In ARCONTIO that perspective is removed entirely.

The world in ARCONTIO exists independently of anyone who observes it. Events happen — an attack, a killing, a theft, a sighting — and they are objective facts. But no character in the simulation has direct access to those facts. Every NPC builds their own version of reality from what they have personally seen, from what they remember, and from what others have told them.

Memory is not a simple log. It is a trace with weight, intensity, and decay. What an NPC knew three days ago may no longer be true. What they heard from a neighbor may already be distorted. What they believe about the world drives every decision they make — and their beliefs are rarely one hundred percent correct.

This is not a bug. It is the system.

What this means in practice:

An NPC walks toward food they remember seeing in a certain place. In the meantime it was moved. They arrive, search, find nothing, and update their map of the world. Confidence in that memory drops. They look for an alternative.

Two NPCs head toward the same resource. Neither knows the other is coming. One arrives first and reserves it. The other is blocked — not by a scripted event, but by the collision of two independent mental models of the same world. The NPC adapts. Remembers.

Information spreads between NPCs, but it degrades. A rumor is not a fact. A warning passed through three people is not the same warning that was originally issued. A crime can remain invisible. An unfounded suspicion can spread as if it were truth.

Social structures — alliances, tensions, informal hierarchies — emerge from this fog of partial knowledge. Not because a designer scripted them, but because characters with different information, acting rationally on what they know, keep colliding with each other.

Between the goals of individual NPCs and their social aggregation there are institutions — justice, trade, city planning — also run by characters with their own biases, experiences, and memories.

The player's role:

You do not control from above. You can inhabit any NPC in the simulation and experience the world through their knowledge, their relationships, their position in society. You might play as a guard who knows only what their patrols have shown them. A merchant with a mental map built from a hundred transactions. An adventurer who knows almost nothing.

You can switch perspectives during the same playthrough. The same event looks different depending on who you are.

ARCONTIO is not built purely to be won. It is built to be observed, influenced — and, with care, shaped: through one absolute heroic protagonist or through a thousand different lives lived to produce your own specific idea of a world.

Prototype in development. Active systems: subjective perception, NPC memory with decay, landmark-based pathfinding, resource reservation, token-based communication.

MANIFESTO — SUBSTACK

Why I'm building a simulation where nobody really knows what's happening

There is a moment in almost every colony sim where everything makes sense from above but nothing makes sense from the inside.

You see the entire map. You know where every resource is. Your colonists execute the tasks you assign them with perfect knowledge of the world you can see but they cannot. The interesting stories — the dwarf who went mad, the colonist who kept wandering into danger — happen despite the system, not because of it. They happen when the designer deliberately introduced some imperfection into an otherwise omniscient simulation.

I kept asking myself: what if the imperfection were not deliberate? What if it were structural?

The premise

ARCONTIO is built on a single idea: events in the world are objective, but the knowledge each character has of those events is not.

An attack happens. It is an objective fact — something that occurred at a specific place, at a specific time, involving specific entities. It carries no moral weight, no emotional content, no built-in interpretation. It is simply what happened.

But no NPC in ARCONTIO has direct access to that fact. What they have is: what they personally witnessed (constrained by line of sight and direction of attention), what they remember of what they witnessed (which fades, distorts, and competes with other memories), and what other NPCs have communicated to them (already filtered through those NPCs' own subjective experience and memory limitations).

The world exists in itself. The mind is subjective. Information is imperfect.

These are the three axioms the simulation runs on.

What changes

In most simulations, the gap between "what is true" and "what characters know" is either zero or carefully scripted. A character does not know about the monster because the designer has not revealed it yet. A colonist receives a negative mood because they witnessed an event the game decided was traumatic.

In ARCONTIO the gap is structural and emergent. Two NPCs can hold conflicting beliefs about the same event not because the designer built a puzzle, but because they were standing in different places when it happened, talked to different people afterward, and carry memories of different ages and different confidence levels.

This means the simulation can generate genuine disagreements about what is true — not only about what is desirable.

I find this more interesting than psychological trait systems, not because psychology is not interesting, but because information asymmetry is a more fundamental source of conflict. You can negotiate with someone who wants different things than you do. It is much harder to negotiate with someone who is operating on a different version of reality.

The technical problem this creates

Everything in the game has to be built twice: once as objective world state, and once as each NPC's subjective model of that state.

Every NPC maintains a personal store of known objects — where they last saw food, where they remember a door being, which path worked last time. Each entry has a confidence level that decays over time and a last-seen timestamp that indicates how stale the memory is. When the world changes and an NPC has not observed the change, their model drifts from reality.

Pathfinding works the same way. NPCs do not navigate on a perfect grid. They navigate on a landmark graph they have built themselves through movement — a compressed mental map of significant points (doorways, junctions) and the connections between them. Two NPCs who have explored different areas have different mental maps of the same space. An NPC who has always worked in one section of the city has no knowledge of shortcuts on the other side.

Communication is also imperfect by design. Information travels between NPCs as symbolic tokens — structured messages carrying intensity, reliability, and a provenance chain. Each time a token passes from one NPC to another it degrades slightly. The further a piece of information travels from its source, the less reliable it becomes. A firsthand account and a third-hand rumor are structurally different objects in this system.

None of this is simple to build. I have rewritten the pathfinding system three times. I am still calibrating the confidence decay rates. The job system has more edge cases than I initially expected. But I keep coming back to these constraints because I think they are the right constraints — they produce behavior that feels genuinely emergent rather than scripted.

What I am trying to observe

ARCONTIO is not built to be won in the classical sense of growing the most prosperous society possible. Beyond the pure sandbox mode — which I call sandbox² because it is a fully open scenario where you can play as any available character — I plan to build structured missions with specific objectives: playing as only a limited set of characters, supporting the growth of a particular faction, or managing a specific institution in a specific way.

All of it is built to observe how social structures emerge from information asymmetry. How informal groups form when multiple NPCs share similar incomplete knowledge. How institutions are managed when individual NPCs cannot resolve conflicts on their own and need coordinating structures. How norms emerge not as preset rules but as patterns reinforced by repeated events and collective memory.

The player does not have god mode. They can inhabit any NPC in the simulation and experience the world through that character's knowledge and relationships. They can switch perspectives — play as the governor, then as a rival candidate (there are elections for that), then as a guild outlaw — and see how the same events look different depending on what you know and who you are.

The society is not the player's interface. It is a system. The player is inside it.

Why I am writing about this

I am building this alone, which means the technical decisions and the design decisions are the same decisions, made by the same person, often at the same time. Writing about them forces me to separate them — to say clearly what I am trying to build and why, before getting too deep into how.

This newsletter is that process made public. Every two weeks or so I will share a technical note on one system in ARCONTIO — how it is designed, what it does, what breaks when it fails. The goal is not documentation. It is thinking in public, which is the only way I know how to think clearly.

If you are interested in simulation design, emergent systems, or what happens when you try to build a society from imperfect information — you are in the right place.

1. Il mondo esiste in sé?

In Arcontio, il mondo esiste prima e a prescindere dei personaggi che lo animano.

Un evento accade indipendentemente dal fatto che qualcuno lo veda.

Un attacco. Una morte. Un furto. Un avvistamento.

Gli eventi sono fatti oggettivi.

Non contengono:

  • giudizi morali
  • emozioni
  • intenzioni

Sono solo ciò che è accaduto.

2. La mente è soggettiva

I personaggi non possono accedere alla verità globale. Possono costruire una loro percezione della realtà a partire da ciò che la loro vista rileva, la loro memoria ricorda e da ciò che altri personaggi comunicano a lui.

La memoria, però, non è un log.

È una traccia con peso, intensità e decadimento.

La realtà sociale nasce da molte verità parziali.

3. L’informazione è imperfetta

In Arcontio non esiste la telepatia.

Ogni informazione è sempre locale, tende a degradarsi, si può distorcere, ma non si propaga da sola.

Un crimine può quindi restare invisibile, mentre un sospetto può diffondersi senza prove.

La società creata in Arcontio non è onnisciente.

4. L’ambiente è un macchina sotto pressione

L’ambiente non è un puzzle che si può ordinare in maniera definitiva. Non è mai completamente controllabile. È una forza dinamica che rompe equilibri, genera scarsità e impone, quindi, delle scelte a volte difficili. La società dei personaggi di Arcontio emerge come risposta alla pressione.

5. Nessuna variabile è puramente positiva

Ogni progresso ha un costo.

Più produzione → più stress.

Più comfort → nuove tensioni.

Più efficienza → nuovi conflitti.

Non esiste equilibrio stabile permanente.

6. Le norme non sono scriptate

Le regole sociali non sono preimpostate come morale universale.

Emergono attraverso una memoria collettiva e un continuo rinforzo determinato dalla ripetizione di eventi. La distribuzione del potere e l’effetto della comunicazione tra personaggi modifica continuamente questa struttura. Un atto può essere tollerato oggi e punito domani.

7. Il giocatore non possiede la verità

Il giocatore non controlla ciò che i personaggi conoscono. Può solo tentare di influenzare alcune condizioni, gli accessi alle risorse e il corpus normativo. Ma non può imporre percezioni.

La società non è la sua interfaccia, è un sistema.

8. Arcontio è un esperimento sistemico

Arcontio non è costruito per ottimizzare punteggi. È costruito per osservare come nascono i conflitti, come si generano i sospetti, come si stabilizzano o si fanno collassare comunità.

È un simulatore di dinamiche sociali.

Versione sintetica (per homepage)

Arcontio è un simulatore sociale rules-driven.

Il mondo produce eventi, e gli eventi vengono vissuti diversamente da ogni personaggio.

Il loro vissuto crea memorie, credenze, sentimenti ed emozioni personali. Questo caleidoscopio contribuisce a stimolare aggregazioni e attriti sociali.

Questa pressione trova una mediazione tramite le istituzioni politiche. Istituzioni che possono essere modulate per risolvere o esacerbare i conflitti.

Il sistema non è isolato: dal singolo alla politica e viceversa, tutto è connesso.

The Manifesto of Arcontio

1. Does the world exist in itself?

In Arcontio, the world exists prior to and independently of the characters who inhabit it.

An event occurs whether or not anyone witnesses it.

An attack. A death. A theft. A sighting.

Events are objective facts.

They contain no:

  • moral judgment
  • emotion
  • intention

They are simply what has happened.

2. The mind is subjective

Characters cannot access global truth.

They construct their own perception of reality from what they see, what they remember, and what others communicate to them.

Memory is not a log.

It is a trace with weight, intensity, and decay.

Social reality emerges from multiple partial truths.

3. Information is imperfect

There is no telepathy in Arcontio.

All information is local. It degrades. It may distort. It does not propagate by itself.

A crime may remain invisible, while suspicion may spread without evidence.

The society simulated in Arcontio is not omniscient.

4. The environment is a machine under pressure

The environment is not a puzzle to be permanently solved.

It is never fully controllable.

It is a dynamic force that disrupts equilibria, generates scarcity, and compels difficult choices.

The society of Arcontio’s characters emerges as a response to pressure.

5. No variable is purely positive

Every advance has a cost.

More production → more stress.

More comfort → new tensions.

More efficiency → new conflicts.

There is no permanent stable equilibrium.

6. Norms are not scripted

Social rules are not pre-written as universal morality.

They emerge through collective memory and are continuously reinforced by the repetition of events.

The distribution of power and the flow of communication constantly reshape this structure.

An act tolerated today may be punished tomorrow.

7. The player does not possess the truth

The player does not control what characters know.

They may influence conditions, access to resources, and the normative framework — but they cannot impose perception.

Society is not the interface. It is a system.

8. Arcontio is a systemic experiment

Arcontio is not built to optimize scores.

It is built to observe how conflicts arise, how suspicion spreads, and how communities stabilize or collapse.

It is a simulator of social dynamics.

Short Version (Homepage)

Arcontio is a rules-driven social simulator.

The world generates events, and each character experiences them differently.

Their lived experiences generate memories, beliefs, emotions, and sentiments.

This kaleidoscope produces social aggregation and friction.

That pressure is mediated through political institutions — institutions that may be adjusted to resolve or exacerbate conflict.

The system is not isolated: from the individual to politics and back again, everything is connected.
